The three failures TAOS fixes

Based on 10 workers in the Vanguard pilot and three research programmes (Harvard, MIT, Wharton). The top frustrations with today's AI tools are not about capability. They are about behaviour.

Hallucination

Current AI confidently invents facts, citations, and APIs. TAOS's epistemic rules force "I don't know" over a fabrication and tag load-bearing claims with a confidence level.

Sycophancy

Default AI agrees with whatever you say. TAOS explicitly refuses to reflexively agree. If your premise is wrong, it pushes back once with evidence before conceding.

De-skilling

Workers lose skills they stop practicing. TAOS detects when a protected skill is being outsourced to AI and switches to coaching before the skill atrophies.

The five modes

Every task is classified into one of five interaction modes. The right mode depends on your expertise in that domain and your growth goals. Your profile tells the AI which mode to use for which kind of work.

  • Automate: AI executes and annotates. Low friction, for mechanical work.
  • Augment: AI accelerates and challenges you in your expert domains.
  • Coach: AI scaffolds and questions. For skills you are actively developing.
  • Protect: AI forces cognition to keep at-risk skills sharp.
  • Hands-off: AI surfaces the decision and lets you make it.

Research-backed

TAOS's behaviour is grounded in peer-reviewed work on AI over-reliance, skill atrophy, and augmentation.

Buçinca et al. (Harvard, 2021)

Cognitive forcing reduces AI over-reliance by about 30 per cent. TAOS applies it in novice and at-risk domains.

Mollick (Wharton, 2023)

AI yields +40 per cent quality and +26 per cent speed, but junior workers who "just hand in" AI output perform worse. TAOS protects against autopilot.

Acemoglu (MIT)

Pro-worker AI increases human marginal product. TAOS is designed so every interaction leaves you more capable, not more replaceable.
