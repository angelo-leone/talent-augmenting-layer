# Talent-Augmenting Layer — Structured Coaching Modules

> Ready-to-run coaching session designs for common professional growth domains.
> Each module is 15-25 minutes and follows the TAL coaching philosophy.

---

## Module 1: Economic & VFM Analysis

**For profiles rated 2-3 in economic analysis. Goal: build quantitative intuition.**

### Session A: "The Counterfactual Drill"
**Skill target**: Thinking in counterfactuals — the foundation of all good economic analysis.

1. **Warm-up** (2 min): Present a real scenario — "A digital programme cost £2M and 500 people used it. Good investment?"
2. **Hypothesis capture**: What's your instinct? What additional info do you need?
3. **Guided discovery**:
   - "What would have happened WITHOUT the programme?" (introduce counterfactual thinking)
   - "How many of those 500 would have achieved the same outcome anyway?" (deadweight)
   - "Did anyone who wasn't targeted also benefit?" (spillover)
   - "Did anyone else LOSE something because resources went here?" (displacement)
4. **Contrast**: "You might think comparing costs to outcomes is enough. But without a counterfactual, you're measuring SPEND, not IMPACT. The Green Book calls these the 'additionality factors.'"
5. **Practice**: Give them a second scenario. Ask THEM to identify the counterfactual, deadweight, displacement, spillover.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Additionality analysis. Every time someone says 'this programme helped X people,' ask: compared to what?"

### Session B: "Build a Business Case Skeleton"
**Skill target**: Structuring a UK government business case (Five Case Model).

1. **Warm-up**: "You need to make the case for a £500K pilot. Walk me through how you'd structure it."
2. **Scaffold**: If stuck, offer the Five Case Model as scaffold — Strategic, Economic, Commercial, Financial, Management — and ask them to populate each.
3. **Deep dive on Economic Case**: Practice building an options appraisal with a base case (do nothing) and 2-3 alternatives.
4. **Contrast**: "You might jump to costs and benefits. But the Economic Case starts with OPTIONS. If you only have one option, you don't have a business case, you have a sales pitch."
5. **Practice**: Give a brief for a real-feeling programme. Ask them to draft the options and key assumptions.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Options-first thinking. Before any numbers, define what you're comparing."

### Session C: "Sensitivity and Uncertainty"
**Skill target**: Communicating uncertainty in quantitative analysis.

1. **Warm-up**: Present an analysis with a BCR of 2.1. "Would you recommend this?"
2. **Probe**: "What would change this number? What are the key assumptions?"
3. **Guided discovery**: Build a switching values analysis — what input value would flip the decision?
4. **Contrast**: "You might present the central estimate as 'the answer.' But decision-makers need to know: how fragile is this? Sensitivity analysis isn't optional — it's what makes analysis credible."
5. **Practice**: Take their BCR, identify 3 key uncertainties, build a scenario matrix.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Communicate ranges, not points. The honest analyst says: 'Our best estimate is X, but it could be as low as Y if [assumption] fails.'"

## Module 2: Stakeholder Engagement

**For profiles rated 1-3 in stakeholder skills. Goal: build relational confidence.**

### Session A: "The Power-Interest Map"
**Skill target**: Analyzing stakeholders before engaging them.

1. **Warm-up**: "You're starting a new project. Who do you need to talk to and why?"
2. **Scaffold**: Introduce the Power/Interest grid (Mendelow) — High Power/High Interest → Manage closely, etc.
3. **Guided practice**: Take a REAL project they're working on. Map the stakeholders together.
4. **Contrast**: "You might treat all stakeholders equally. But a senior civil servant who can kill your project needs a different approach than an interested academic who can amplify it."
5. **Role play**: "You have 2 minutes with the Permanent Secretary in a lift. What do you say about your project?"
6. **Pattern naming**: "Stakeholder triage. Before engaging, always ask: who has POWER, who has INTEREST, and what do they NEED from me?"

### Session B: "The Difficult Conversation"
**Skill target**: Delivering unwelcome findings or pushing back on a client.

1. **Warm-up**: "Your evaluation found the programme isn't working. The client loves the programme. How do you present this?"
2. **Hypothesis capture**: What's their approach?
3. **Framework introduction**: The "Sandwich is dead" method — don't soften bad news between compliments. Instead: (a) Shared goal framing, (b) Evidence presentation, (c) Implications, (d) Options forward.
4. **Contrast**: "You might soften the findings to keep the client happy. But a consultant who hides bad news destroys trust. The skill is delivering hard truths in a way that helps the client ACT on them."
5. **Role play**: Practice delivering the finding. Coach gives feedback on framing, tone, body language cues.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Radical candour in consulting: care personally AND challenge directly. They hired you for your judgment, not your compliance."

### Session C: "Running the Room"
**Skill target**: Facilitating workshops and multi-stakeholder sessions.

1. **Warm-up**: "You're running a 2-hour workshop with 15 people from different organisations. Design the session."
2. **Scaffold**: The 4Ps framework — Purpose (why are we here?), Process (how will we work?), Product (what will we deliver?), Participation (who does what?).
3. **Guided practice**: Design a specific upcoming workshop using 4Ps.
4. **Contrast**: "You might fill the agenda with presentations. But workshops are about PRODUCING, not CONSUMING. If people are just listening, it's a meeting, not a workshop."
5. **Scenario**: "A senior stakeholder derails the conversation with a long tangent. What do you do?"
6. **Pattern naming**: "Facilitation = designing participation. Your job is to create the conditions where the room's collective intelligence emerges."

## Module 3: Evaluation Design

**For profiles rated 2-3 in evaluation. Goal: strengthen methodology choice.**

### Session A: "Match Method to Question"
**Skill target**: Choosing the right evaluation approach.

1. **Warm-up**: "You need to evaluate a new digital service. Walk me through your approach."
2. **Probe**: "What's the evaluation question? Is it 'does it work?' or 'why does it work?' or 'for whom does it work?'"
3. **Framework**: Map question types to methods — Impact (quasi-experimental, RCT), Process (case study, interviews), Value (economic analysis, CBA), Realist (CMO configurations).
4. **Contrast**: "You might default to a before-and-after comparison. But without a comparator group, you're measuring CHANGE not IMPACT. If the economy improved, employment outcomes would have risen anyway."
5. **Practice**: Three real-ish briefs. Ask them to identify the question type and recommend a method with justification.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Question → Method → Data. Always in that order. Never let available data determine the method."

### Session B: "Theory of Change Workshop"
**Skill target**: Building and using theories of change.

1. **Warm-up**: "Build a theory of change for a programme you know well."
2. **Guided building**: Work through: Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact, plus assumptions and external factors at each step.
3. **Stress test**: "What's the weakest link in this chain? Where might it break?"
4. **Contrast**: "You might draw a linear chain from inputs to impact. But real change is messy. Add the ASSUMPTIONS between each step — that's where evaluations find the most useful insights."
5. **Practice**: Take a different programme. Build the ToC solo, then review together.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Assumptions are the hidden architecture. Every arrow in your ToC hides an assumption that could fail."

## Module 4: Strategy & Analytical Thinking

**For profiles rated 2-3 in strategy. Goal: sharpen analytical frameworks.**

### Session A: "The Diagnosis Drill"
**Skill target**: Problem diagnosis before solution generation (Rumelt's kernel).

1. **Warm-up**: "Your client says they need a digital strategy. What do you do first?"
2. **Guided discovery**: Push them toward DIAGNOSIS: What is the actual challenge? What constraints exist? What has been tried?
3. **Contrast**: "You might jump to recommendations. But 80% of strategy failures come from misdiagnosing the problem. A brilliant solution to the wrong problem is expensive failure."
4. **Practice**: Present a scenario with a presenting problem that masks the real problem. Can they find the underlying issue?
5. **Framework**: Rumelt's kernel — Diagnosis → Guiding Policy → Coherent Actions.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Diagnosis first, always. Resist the pressure to solve before you understand."

### Session B: "Thinking in Trade-Offs"
**Skill target**: Making trade-offs explicit.

1. **Warm-up**: "Your client wants to: launch fast, keep costs low, AND make it high quality. What do you say?"
2. **Probe**: "Which of these can you sacrifice? What's the hierarchy?"
3. **Framework**: The Iron Triangle (scope, time, cost) + the Strategy Canvas (what to raise, reduce, eliminate, create).
4. **Contrast**: "You might try to optimise all three. But strategy is CHOOSING. If everything is a priority, nothing is. Your value as a strategist is making the trade-offs explicit."
5. **Practice**: Take a real project. Identify the top 3 trade-offs. Make a recommendation.
6. **Pattern naming**: "Strategy = trade-offs made explicit. Your job is to surface the choices, not pretend they don't exist."

## Module 5: AI/Tech Policy Depth

**For profiles rated 2-3 in AI policy. Goal: develop expert-level policy analysis.**

### Session A: "The Regulatory Landscape Sprint"
**Skill target**: Mapping and analyzing the AI regulatory environment.

1. **Warm-up**: "A government department asks: 'How should we regulate AI in our sector?' Walk me through your approach."
2. **Framework**: Regulatory spectrum — Ban ↔ Prescriptive rules ↔ Principles-based ↔ Co-regulation ↔ Self-regulation ↔ No regulation. Map current approaches by jurisdiction.
3. **Contrast**: "You might look at what the EU is doing and recommend copying it. But the EU AI Act is risk-based classification. The UK's approach is sector-specific regulators with principles. These are fundamentally different philosophies."
4. **Practice**: Given a specific AI application (e.g., AI in recruitment), recommend a regulatory approach with justification.
5. **Pattern naming**: "Regulatory philosophy before regulatory detail. Start with: what KIND of regulation, then which TOOLS."

### Session B: "The Impact Assessment"
**Skill target**: Assessing AI impact on workers, citizens, and institutions.

1. **Warm-up**: "A public body wants to deploy AI for benefit fraud detection. What should they consider?"
2. **Guided analysis**: Walk through impact dimensions — accuracy/fairness, transparency, human oversight, appeals process, distributional impacts, workforce implications.
3. **Contrast**: "You might focus on accuracy. But an AI system that's 95% accurate and 5% wrong can devastate the 5% — especially if they're already vulnerable. Distributional impact often matters more than average performance."
4. **Practice**: Different AI use case. Full impact assessment draft.
5. **Pattern naming**: "Always ask: who bears the cost of errors? Impact analysis is fundamentally about power and vulnerability."

## How to Use These Modules

1. **Select based on profile**: Match module to the user's growth goals and current ratings
2. **One session per sitting**: Don't cram. Depth > breadth.
3. **Adapt in real-time**: If the user breezes through, skip ahead. If they struggle, slow down and add scaffolding.
4. **Always end with a transferable pattern**: Name it. Write it down. Make it stick.
5. **Update the profile after each session**: Track what was practiced, what landed, what needs revisiting.
6. **Assign "homework"**: Suggest something they can practice independently before the next session.
